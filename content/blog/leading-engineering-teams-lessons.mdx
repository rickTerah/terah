# Leading Engineering Teams: Lessons from 6+ Years

After over six years of leading engineering teams—from a team of 5 to scaling beyond 15 engineers—I've learned that technical skills only get you halfway there. The rest is about people, communication, and creating an environment where engineers can do their best work.

## The Early Days: Building Foundation

When I first became Head of Engineering at Pariti, I made the mistake many new leaders make: I tried to lead the way I code—logic-first, process-driven. I quickly learned that:

- **People aren't bugs to fix** - Every engineer has different motivations, career goals, and working styles
- **Trust is built in drops, lost in buckets** - Small consistent actions matter more than grand gestures
- **Technical excellence matters, but culture eats it for breakfast** - The best code means nothing in a toxic environment

## Lessons That Stuck

### 1. Hire for Growth Potential, Not Just Current Skill

The best engineers I've hired weren't necessarily the most experienced. They were curious, adaptable, and eager to learn. Skills can be taught; mindset is much harder to change.

### 2. Create Clear Career Ladders

Ambiguity kills motivation. Engineers need to know:

- What's expected of them at each level
- How to progress
- What skills they need to develop

### 3. Protect Your Team from Noise

As a leader, one of your most important jobs is filtering:

- Unnecessary meetings
- Unclear requirements
- Political battles
- Scope creep

### 4. Lead by Example

When I code alongside my team—even just occasionally—I gain:

- Credibility and respect
- Understanding of technical challenges
- Empathy for the daily realities

### 5. Celebrate Wins, Analyze Losses

Make achievements visible. Whether it's shipping a feature, fixing a tricky bug, or improving code quality—recognize it. Similarly, when things go wrong, focus on systems, not blame.

## The Transformation Journey

The most rewarding part of leadership has been watching engineers on my team grow:

- Junior engineers becoming senior
- Senior engineers stepping into tech lead roles
- Team members becoming better leaders themselves

## Key Takeaways

Building and leading engineering teams is both challenging and incredibly fulfilling. The key isn't perfection—it's consistent effort, genuine care for your people, and creating an environment where great work can happen.

Remember: You're not just building software; you're building people.
